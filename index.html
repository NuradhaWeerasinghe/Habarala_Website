Literature Survey










Since, Sri Lanka continues to suffer from rising food prices as a result of the pandemic and economic crisis, with 6.26 million people food insecure and 65,600 severely food insecure. To support the zero-hunger goal, this research aims to address two main aspects using a novel technological approach: introducing and promoting alternative food resources, and uplifting plantation, agriculture, and home gardening. The issue of food scarcity in Sri Lanka is often caused by the lack of knowledge about available edible plants in the local environment, despite a wide range of such plants being present. This leads to an over-reliance on expensive and popular crops. The root cause of this problem is a lack of knowledge and expertise, which impedes home gardening and agriculture. Even though people tend to grow their own food, they struggle due to the lack of expertise. They struggle to manage their space and identify optimal plants to grow according to their requirements. However, the absence of a system that can bridge this gap is the main challenge.


To address this issue, the proposed system uses the latest technologies such as Deep Learning (DL), Machine Learning (ML), and Augmented Reality (AR). By using these technologies,‘Habarala’ provides users, with an efficient way to identify unconventional or underutilized edible plants in their immediate environment that can be used to prepare day-today meals by using provided recipes, nutritional, and general information. To familiarize users with green technology, the solution assists users in selecting the most suitable plants to grow based on their geographical location and climate facts such as weather, soil type, and available space. Before cultivating a selected plant, with the ‘Habarala,’ users can evaluate whether the selected area is enough or not to grow and flourish the plant with the space evaluation along with a 3D augmented preview. Additionally, the application provides a platform for users to find and buy crops, seeds, fertilizers, and supportive tools they need in one convenient place. Ultimately, this smart solution empowers individuals to become more self-sufficient in their food production while contributing to the global effort to eradicate hunger and promote sustainable agriculture.

Research Objectives

Alternative edible Plant identification & Recipe Suggestions

Identification of plants using image classification. Identification of the best transfer learning based CNN architecture for differentiate the plant with a better accuracy. Provide recipe suggestions for the resulting plant. Extracting food recipes for the selected alternative edible plants

Plantation Recommendation based on climate conditions

Povide personalized plant recommendations for the user based on climate trends. Collect climate data factors for selected alternative plants. Find the best machine learning (ML) model to recommend the plant with a better accuracy

Space Evaluation and Modeling of the Plant Preview Using Augmented Reality

Identify the plane and get the measurements accurately using augment Reality and calculate the area. Validate the area and generate correct responses. Position the 3D model in the area and display the augmented output. Suggest the optimal way of managing the space for the selected plants

Marketplace and Recommendation system

Predict the angle (left, right, middle, up, down) from eye gaze data. Collect the buying patterns according to the user's previous data and rank the product. Build personalized product recommendation model based on eye gaze and behavioral data. Implement the mobile application and Integrate the model to the mobile app.

Methodology










The overall process of the proposed application is illustrated in Figure 1. It consists of the following four features to achieve the research objectives, Discover Edible Plants Through Leaf Identification, Plantation Recommendation based on Climate Conditions, Augmented Reality (AR) for Space Evaluation and 3D Modeling, and Personalized Personalized Product Recommendation.


A newly captured data set was collected from four different alternative edible plants to train the deep-learning neural network models. The chosen four edible plants were Cassava, Jackfruit, Mango, and Passionfruit, which were carefully selected with the assistance of the Department of Agriculture and the Department of Ayurveda of Sri Lanka. The leaf Images were captured, with care taken to ensure the diversity of the images. The leaf image data set was divided into two subsets, with 4778 images allocated for training and 1197 images allocated for testing. The ratio of images between the training and testing sets was 8:2, respectively.


To create a plant recommendation for alternative plants, machine learning models were trained using a dataset that consists of environmental factors of plants. The obtained dataset includes 11,236 records of important factors such as NPK(Nitrogen Phosphorus and Potassium) levels, plant information, soil pH, humidity, and temperature. The data includes 84 plant types collected from different locations to increase the variety of the dataset. After data collection, data preprocessing techniques were applied to clean the dataset, improve accuracy, and reduce complexity. The dataset contained both categorical and numerical columns, requiring encoding techniques to convert categorical columns into numerical values. The dataset was not scaled, making it difficult to identify outliers, which encouraged the use of a scaling technique. Feature engineering was also used to extract features from unprocessed data using domain knowledge to improve Machine Learning (ML) output.


The process includes two main sections: space evaluation and augmenting plant previews. The first part involves assessing whether the provided space is sufficient for the plant or not. This will be done through an application where the user can select the desired plant and mark the space available for planting using the displayed markers on the screen. The application will then calculate the area and evaluate whether it meets the minimum space required for the selected plant. The second part involves augmenting a 3D view of the selected plant in the available area if the space is sufficient. To achieve this, a specific data set about plants is needed, which includes details about average plant height, average space required to grow, and high-quality plant images which covers every angle of the plant. Data was collected for 84 plants with the assistance of the Department of Agriculture and the Department of Ayurveda of Sri Lanka.


Here, the primary objective was to develop a food, plant, and agricultural items recommendation for the consumers. To achieve this, a content-based and feature-based model along with eye gaze detection were developed to track the consumer’s personalized preferences. Both Deep-learning (DL) and Machine Learning (ML) approaches were used to develop this Personalized Product Recommendation(PPR).
